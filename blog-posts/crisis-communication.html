Kolkata’s Economic Slide: From Glory to Crisis

Nestled on the banks of the Hooghly River, Kolkata was once the beating heart of India’s economy. In the early 1960s, West Bengal (then led by Calcutta) contributed 10.5 % to the nation’s GDP and boasted a per‑capita income 27.5 % above the national average.Fast-forward to 2023–24, and that share has shrunk nearly in half—to just 5.6 %, with per‑capita income languishing at 83.7 % of the national average.

1. Industrial Collapse & Policy Paralysis

Once a textile and jute powerhouse with a busy port and colonial-era infrastructure, Kolkata’s industry began unwinding in the 1960s. Labour unrest, militant unionism, power shortages, and political instability resulted in industrial closures. The famed Tata Nano project at Singur collapsed amid land-acquisition strife, prompting Tata to relocate to Gujarat—resulting in a boom there but a missed opportunity here

2. Missed the Liberalisation Wave

While southern and western states embraced economic reforms post-1991, West Bengal lagged. The EAC‑PM paper highlights that states like Tamil Nadu and Odisha pulled ahead, while West Bengal fell further behind

3. Infrastructure & Governance Gaps

Persistent problems — inadequate power, poor road networks, bureaucratic bottlenecks, and syndicate-led extortion (“Syndicate Raj”) — have deterred investment. Attracted FDI is minimal: West Bengal drew only US $1.6 bn from 2019–2023, compared to tens of billions in Maharashtra or Karnataka.

4. Capital Flight & Job Drain

More than 2,200 firms reportedly moved out between 2019 and 2024 . Reddit users lament that “companies left for Karnataka,” and IT firms relocated due to Bengal’s anti‑expansionist policies

Voices from the Ground

“West Bengal had the highest per capita income among large states in 1961… In 2018‑19, West Bengal was poorer than Chhattisgarh, an impoverished state”

“Companies left for Karnataka… lack of land to expand the city”

These reflect deep local concerns: industrial stagnation, migration of skilled youth, and missed opportunities.

Broader Economic Fallout

GDP & Employment

Per‑GSDP growth slowed from ~5.8 % in 2018 to ~4 % in 2024. Budget deficits soared—revenue fell by ₹25,000 Cr, pushing the fiscal gap toward ₹73,000 Cr and squeezing capital investment.

Social & Demographic Impacts

Unemployment rises as industry shrinks. Urban migration of skilled workers continues, reducing Kolkata’s competitiveness. The real estate and construction slump, tied partly to corruption and syndicate control, exacerbates civic stagnation

Seeds of Revival

Despite serious headwinds, there are glimmers of hope:

Fiscal Discipline: Own-tax revenue has quadrupled since 2010, capital spending is rising, and fiscal deficit is contracting .

Policy Pushes: Initiatives such as the New Town IT hub (₹27 000 Cr projected investment), Bengal Global Business Summit, and MSME credit targets signal intent to improve investment climates .

Conclusion

Kolkata's economic story is one of decline—from one of India's leading industrial and economic hubs to a region struggling to keep pace. The fall in GDP share from 10.5 % to 5.6 %, coupled with per‑capita levels falling below laggards like Odisha, is stark. While recent fiscal and policy measures offer hope, reversing decades of decline demands sustained structural reform—prioritising industrial revival, infrastructure modernization, business-friendliness, and human capital development.However, substantial reforms are still needed: streamlined governance, land policy reform, infrastructure upgrade, relief for small industries, and formal vocational training pathways .

Kolkata’s future depends on its willingness to embrace change after long years of complacency. With smart governance and honest investment in people and infrastructure, it might yet reclaim a place among India’s economic frontrunners.
